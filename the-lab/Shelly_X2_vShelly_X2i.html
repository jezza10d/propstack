Form vs. Friction: A Brutal Reality Check on the Shelly X2 & X2i.

From Gen1 relays to premium glass wall panels. Evaluating Shelly's flagships when stripped of Android containers, cloud bloat, and standard dashboard bottlenecks.

THEPROPSTACK JUNE 16, 2026

My relationship with Shelly isn’t new. I’ve been close to the brand since the early days of their Gen1 ecosystem, back when the little round, orange Shelly H&T (Humidity & Temperature) sensor was the best way to get cheap, local telemetry into a home server. I watched them scale through the classic blue Shelly 1 relays to the Pro series. So when the **Shelly X2** and **X2i** wall switches landed, I wanted them to be the definitive answer to my frontend interface needs.

Before this, I had a couple of Sonoff NSPanel Pro units floating around the infrastructure. I went down the usual rabbit hole with them: rooting the hardware, flashing Android, stripping background resource hogs, and running custom APKs like Fully Kiosk Browser. They worked fine, but they carried those endless micro-annoyances common with running heavy operating systems on low-tier silicon.

The shift away from the standard Home Assistant companion app over to a lightweight, local HTML webpage dashboard cleared out most of that lag. But hardware limitations can't always be patched out in software. Here is how the X2 series actually handles real-world deployment.

Physical Deployment & Proportions

Let’s look at the physical layout first. Proportionally, the X2 line is a significant upgrade over the smaller, cramped Shelly wall switches. The faceplate presence is cleaner, less industrial, and frames a space properly.

However, physics always wins when it comes to installation. You will need a **deep back-box** to house these units comfortably, and a **dedicated neutral wire** is non-negotiable. I anticipated this requirement during the infrastructure phase of my recent basement build, ensuring clean, deep boxes and full neutrals were pulled to every node before plastering.

It's worth noting that while the X2 units feature integrated relays and secondary environment sensors, I completely ignore them. Because every light fixture and load in my environment is managed down-line by its own dedicated dimmer module, these premium wall plates exist purely as **networked input controllers**.

The App Bottleneck: Why the X2i Falters

In production, the standard **Shelly X2** performs reasonably well, but the intelligent **X2i** introduces clear frustrations. When forced to interact through the standard Home Assistant dashboard ecosystem, execution feels sluggish—commands feel heavy, and state changes take too long to update on-screen.

Worse, treating Home Assistant as a native app shell introduces systemic breaks. Basic notification pipelines—like standard notify.notify actions—frequently fail to resolve correctly within the container.

The Workaround: Dropping the native app entirely and running a raw, optimized HTML webpage frontend on the panels stabilizes performance, but it isn't a perfect cure. The experience transitions from "frustrating" to "acceptable," but the underlying hardware still introduces friction.

The underlying issue is tactile. The glass capacitive touch response is simply **not as sensitive as it should be**. It feels clumsy to manage in high-velocity zones. If you hit the switch off-center or with a light touch, it occasionally misses the register, requiring a second, deliberate press. For high-end architectural hardware, that tactile hesitation breaks the fluid user experience.

Funky Routing: The UniFi Protect Doorbell Webhook

To make these switches pull their weight, you have to look past the default software boundaries and build custom logic loops. One of the cooler automations I’ve engineered bridges my security perimeter straight into the kitchen and hallway panels via local webhooks.

When someone steps up and presses the **UniFi Protect G4 Doorbell Pro**, a localized automation script intercepts the ring event in Home Assistant. Instead of relying on a standard smart speaker announcement, it fires an instant, low-latency webhook directly to the local IP addresses of the X2 and X2i wall plates. This triggers one of Shelly's native onboard chime sounds directly out of the internal speaker of the switch itself.

// unifi_doorbell_shelly_webhook.yaml 
alias: "Perimeter: Doorbell Press -> Shelly Wall Chime"
trigger:
  - platform: state
    entity_id: binary_sensor.doorbell_pro_button
    to: "on"
action:
  - parallel:
      - service: uri_request.post
        data:
          url: "http://10.0.30.55/rpc/X2.PlaySound?id=1"
      - service: uri_request.post
        data:
          url: "http://10.0.30.56/rpc/X2i.PlaySound?id=1"
mode: single

The Verdict

The Shelly X2 and X2i occupy a strange middle-ground. They easily beat out the hacked, resource-heavy Sonoff setups once you pair them with a clean HTML frontend. Their physical size is excellent, and if you plan your infrastructure early with deep back-boxes and solid power delivery, they sit neatly in a premium architectural space.

But they require you to design around their flaws. If you rely on native Home Assistant app dashboards or expect highly sensitive touch response from a casual tap, their clumsy interface characteristics will show up quickly. Treat them purely as local, scriptable webhook targets and custom HTML web view windows, and they become a solid, if slightly stubborn, piece of edge gear.
