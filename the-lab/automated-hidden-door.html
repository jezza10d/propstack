Engineering a Concealed Automated Stone Wall Door.

How I bridged heavy structural masonry with micro-edge relay automation to construct a completely hidden, motor-driven utility entrance.

Building high-end smart infrastructure on a sensible budget means getting your hands dirty with raw physics before you even touch a terminal. The challenge here was deceptively simple but structurally brutal: build a fully concealed swing door seamless with a rugged exterior stone veneer wall, and automate it so it operates via local-first API triggers without tearing its own hinges apart.

The Masonry Problem: Weight & Substrate

When dealing with real stone, weight is your worst enemy. Standard timber framings or drywall backings would flex under the lateral shear, inevitably breaking the stone-to-stone joints across the boundary line. To defeat this, I structured the door frame around heavy gauge steel pivoting brackets anchored squarely into the surrounding concrete sub-frame.

For the cladding face of the door itself, normal plywood or plasterboard was out of the question due to damp resistance and structural rigidity requirements. Instead, I deployed a high-density concrete backer board. This provided an inflexible, non-porous canvas capable of bonding perfectly with mortar while dealing gracefully with the dynamic torque of mechanical operations.

Mechanical Drive: The Olide Opener

To handle the significant mass of the completed masonry block, I sourced an Olide automatic residential swing door opener. These commercial-grade units are engineered with robust internal gearing and adjustable torque limits, ensuring the heavy swinging slab starts smoothly and decelerates elegantly without shaking loose the stone veneer layer.

The Edge Integration: Shelly Uni

Out of the box, the door operator expects proprietary remote fobs or wired wall buttons. To align this with a secure-by-design local-first ecosystem, I intercepted the low-voltage control lines on the main logic board using a Shelly Uni microchip relay.

The Shelly Uni is ideal here because of its tiny footprint and clean dry-contact execution. Powered directly by the opener's internal DC accessory rail, it allows me to monitor the door's state inputs while providing clear MQTT trigger arrays to bridge structural access with my home server architecture.

The finished execution passes the ultimate barrier: the Wife Approval Factor. With zero visible gaps, flush stone seams matching the adjacent masonry line, and reliable network actuation, the portal functions as an invisible, silent vault entry point built at a fraction of standard architectural automation costs.
