The Backstory: Escaping the Synology Sandbox

For a long time, my Home Assistant setup was running inside a Virtual Machine on my Synology NAS. It did the job, but virtualization is a compromise for people who enjoy mediocrity. It comes with caveats—resource sharing, USB pass-through quirks, and the constant underlying anxiety that a heavy NAS task might lag my core automation logic.

When you're managing a smart home ecosystem with hundreds of devices, custom dashboards, and highly complex automation scripts tracking everything from regional telemetry to real-time power metrics, a laggy VM isn't just an inconvenience; it's a structural failure. It was time to give the smart home its own dedicated space.

Space wasn't an issue. After wrapping up a massive year-long basement refurbishment project, I ended up building a dedicated control room with a full 6ft network cabinet, leaving plenty of empty U-space in the rack waiting to be filled.

The goal for this build was strict: it had to be entirely fanless while maintaining aggressive passive cooling. Because the control room is tied directly into the home’s MVHR (Mechanical Ventilation with Heat Recovery) extraction system, I can pull any ambient warmth generated by the rack and recycle it right back into the house. No noisy server fans required—just clean, passive heat recovery. It's efficient, silent, and intellectually superior.

Phase 1: The Design, The Market Panic, and The Industrial Overkill

Designing a completely fanless, rackmountable powerhouse isn't as straightforward as picking parts out of a consumer catalog.

Is an i5 Thin-ITX build absolute overkill for a smart home hub? Without a doubt. The whole project is completely, hilariously overpowered for what it's doing today, but I wanted serious headroom to grow into over the next decade. To be clear: this will never be an AI server—local LLMs and heavy computer vision will eventually live on their own dedicated hardware down the line. This machine is built to be a bulletproof, ultra-stable traffic controller for core network infrastructure.

But getting the design from paper to the rack turned into a high-stakes experiment.

My first major misstep was ordering a high-performance, notoriously volatile SSD. While the drive was blindingly fast on paper, its aggressive power states and extreme thermal spikes simply wouldn't balance with the strict, conservative power delivery of the industrial Kontron board. It was a mismatch made in hardware hell.

Market Disruption Warning: While troubleshooting the volatile drive and second-guessing my component balancing, semiconductor and memory chip prices started spiking in real time. Watching tickers and component costs tick upward day by day while sitting on an incomplete build adds a special kind of pressure. I ended up cutting my losses on the volatile drive and pivoting to a rock-solid, predictable Kingston NVMe SSD—paying the premium to secure the rest of the silicon before the market completely detached from reality.

Once the dust settled on the market chaos, everything went smoothly with the physical assembly—until I hit a double wall: a classic industrial-to-consumer cable mismatch on the front ports, followed by the specific hurdle of flashing a dedicated Home Assistant Operating System (HAOS) onto an x86 internal NVMe drive.

Phase 2: The 2.0mm vs. 2.54mm Internal USB Mismatch

In standard consumer tech, internal USB 2.0 headers are predictable. They are chunky, 9-pin black plastic blocks with a standard pitch of 2.54mm. The modular USB front-panel PCB inside the Akasa Galileo MK2 case (labeled A-NUC38-USB2.0) expects exactly that: a standard 9-pin interface.

But industrial Thin-ITX boards like the Kontron play by different rules. Space is at an absolute premium. Instead of a standard consumer header, Kontron exposes a specialized, single-row 5-pin mini-header with a tiny 2.00mm pitch. If you try to mate a standard case block to those micro-pins, it physically won't fit. You can’t swap the case header easily, and you can’t change the motherboard pins. You have to swap the line in between.

The Fix: To bridge the Akasa module to the Kontron board cleanly without stacking clunky adapters inside a tight, fanless chassis, you replace the stock line with a true Hybrid Internal USB 2.0 Cable. It requires a 10-pin/9-pin Female (2.54mm) on one end for the case module, and a 5-pin Female (2.0mm) on the other for the board.

[ ⚠️ WIRING MATRIX WARNING ]
If you ever manually pin this using individual jumper wires, you must map the lines flawlessly:
Red (+5V) → White (Data-) → Green (Data+) → Black (GND)
Misaligning the power and ground on an industrial board will permanently destroy the USB controller. Don't blame me if you create an expensive brick.

Phase 3: Flashing HAOS onto Bare Metal (The Right Way)

With the physical wiring sorted, the antennas snapped onto the Intel AX210 (the Bluetooth lead secured to the AUX port on the right side of the card for clean sensor tracking), and the power connected, it was time for the software stack.

The goal is to run HAOS (Home Assistant Operating System) directly on the bare-metal x86 hardware. Running HAOS natively gives the system raw access to the i5’s processing power, keeps overhead at zero, and ensures storage writes to the Kingston drive are handled efficiently.

However, you cannot simply download an installer file onto a USB stick and run a typical Windows-style wizard to target an internal NVMe drive. HAOS is distributed as a pre-baked disk image. To write that image directly onto the internal drive, we have to use an elegant workaround: loading a live Linux environment first.

[Main PC] ---> Flash Ubuntu Live to USB Drive | v [New Server] -> Boot Ubuntu from USB (Run in RAM) | v [Ubuntu Desktop] -> Download HAOS Image -> Flash directly onto Internal NVMe SSD

Step-by-Step Pipeline Execution

[ STEP 01: PREPARE THE LIVE UBUNTU USB ]

On your primary desktop or laptop, download a tool to write raw disk images, along with a portable operating system:

  • Download balenaEtcher (or Rufus) onto your working computer.
  • Download the standard Ubuntu Desktop ISO (a live Linux environment that can run completely out of your system's RAM).
  • Insert a spare USB flash drive, open balenaEtcher, select the Ubuntu ISO, choose your flash drive, and hit Flash!

[ STEP 02: BOOT INTO THE LIVE ENVIRONMENT ]

Take the freshly flashed Ubuntu USB stick and insert it into one of the working blue USB 3.0 ports on the rear I/O of the server.

  • Connect a keyboard, mouse, and monitor, then apply power.
  • Tap F2 (or Delete) immediately on startup to drop into the Kontron UEFI BIOS.
  • Verify your hardware states: check that your CPU is making flawless contact with the fanless chassis heatsink block and that temps are stable.
  • Navigate to the Boot menu, set your USB flash drive as the primary boot target, save settings, and exit.
  • When the boot menu appears, choose "Try Ubuntu" (Do not click install). This loads a full, usable Linux desktop running entirely inside the system's RAM, leaving your internal Kingston SSD completely clean and unmounted.

[ STEP 03: WRITE THE RAW HAOS IMAGE TO THE INTERNAL SSD ]

Now that you are sitting at the temporary Ubuntu desktop, open the built-in Firefox browser.

  • Head straight to the official Home Assistant documentation and grab the latest URL for the Generic x86-64 (.img.xz) Home Assistant OS image.
  • Open the Ubuntu terminal, or simply download balenaEtcher for Linux right inside your live session.
  • Fire up balenaEtcher within the temporary Ubuntu environment.
  • Select Flash from URL and paste the direct HAOS link you copied, or select the file if you downloaded it locally.
  • For the target drive, select your internal Kingston NVMe SSD (Double-check the storage size to make absolutely sure you aren't selecting your live USB stick!).
  • Click Flash! and let it write the raw operating system image directly onto the bare metal of the drive.

Phase 4: The First Cold Boot

Once balenaEtcher completes the write cycle, shut down the system completely. Pull the Ubuntu live USB stick out of the port so the board doesn't try to boot back into Linux.

Power the system back up. The Kontron board will initialize, scan the storage controllers, detect the native UEFI boot partitions that HAOS just laid down on the NVMe drive, and launch straight into the Home Assistant command line interface.

Within two minutes, the terminal will print out your local network IP address. From there, you can drop back onto your main laptop, type that IP address into your browser with port :8123, and watch the initial provisioning screen fire up.

The server is silent, the front ports are tied in perfectly via our hybrid cable swap, and the bare-metal architecture is primed for deployment. Next up: mapping out the network segments and pulling the local MQTT broker online to manage the hundreds of devices waiting for their new traffic controller. Try not to mess it up.
